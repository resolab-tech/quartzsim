vo/vi — magnitude & phase vs frequency

Answer — does the oscillator settle at fL, or anywhere in the −180° band?

Between fr and fp the feedback network's phase shift stays close to −180° — over roughly the pullability band (fp − fs). Combined with the inverter's own −180°, the Barkhausen phase condition is satisfied over this whole band. That alone is not enough to set the oscillation frequency — the gain condition has to be met simultaneously.

Steady-state oscillation requires both:

  1. ∠(−A · H(jω)) = 0 (mod 360°) — loop phase condition, and
  2. |−A · H(jω)| = 1 — loop gain condition (unity at the settled amplitude).

The phase condition is satisfied over a narrow band, but the gain magnitude |H(jω)| is strongly peaked inside that band. In a Pierce with an ideal transconductance inverter, the gain peak aligns with the frequency where the crystal's reactance exactly cancels 1/(jωCL), i.e.:

Im{ Zx(jω) + 1/(jωCL) } = 0 ⇒ ω = ωL

That single frequency is exactly fL by its own definition. So the oscillator does not smear across the 51.5 → 65.33 ppm band — it locks to one specific frequency, and in the ideal-inverter limit that frequency is fL.

In a real circuit the settled frequency deviates from fL by a small, calculable pull:

  • Finite inverter output resistance ro adds a series loss that effectively increases Rm. Since phase slope through fL is extremely steep (Q ≈ 2.7·10⁴), this changes the frequency by only a few tens of ppb.
  • Inverter internal phase lag φinv (from CMOS pole, not exactly −180°) shifts the frequency so the feedback phase compensates: Δf/f ≈ φinv/(2·Qloaded). For a well-designed 27 MHz Pierce this is typically < 1 ppm.
  • Stray parasitics on C1, C2 effectively change CL and so move fL itself — the Cstray input above lets you see this directly.

Bottom line: the oscillator settles at one, sharply-defined frequency that is essentially fL. Any frequency in the "phase ≈ −180° band" that is not also a gain-peak and not also a stable fixed point of the loop equation is rejected — the crystal's huge Q makes the selection extremely sharp (kHz-scale phase window collapses to <1 Hz oscillation line).

(In the plot above you can see |H| peaks very close to fL, not at fs or fp; this is the gain-peak that selects the oscillation frequency.)

Derivation — fs, fp, fL from first principles (Rm included, no lossless approximation)

1. Crystal impedance Zx(jω)

Motional branch: $Z_m = R_m + j X_m$ with $X_m(\omega)=\omega L_m - 1/(\omega C_m)$.

Static shunt: $Z_0 = 1/(j\omega C_0) = j X_0$ with $X_0 = -1/(\omega C_0)$.

Parallel combination:

$$Z_x(j\omega) \;=\; Z_m \parallel Z_0 \;=\; \frac{Z_m\, Z_0}{Z_m + Z_0} \;=\; \frac{(R_m + j X_m)(j X_0)}{R_m + j(X_m + X_0)}$$

Multiplying numerator and denominator by the conjugate $R_m - j(X_m + X_0)$ yields the exact real and imaginary parts:

$$\operatorname{Re}\{Z_x\} \;=\; \frac{R_m\, X_0^{\,2}}{R_m^{\,2} + (X_m + X_0)^{\,2}}$$ $$\operatorname{Im}\{Z_x\} \;=\; \frac{X_0\,\bigl(X_m^{\,2} + X_m X_0 + R_m^{\,2}\bigr)}{R_m^{\,2} + (X_m + X_0)^{\,2}}$$

These hold for the full 4-element model — no $R_m\!=\!0$ assumption has been used.

2. Pierce feedback transfer function

In a Pierce topology the two load caps C1 (gate-GND) and C2 (drain-GND) appear in series through ground as seen from the crystal's two terminals. Including a stray $C_s$ split evenly:

$$C_L \;=\; \frac{(C_1 + C_s/2)\,(C_2 + C_s/2)}{C_1 + C_2 + C_s}$$

The crystal in series with this equivalent $C_L$ drives a divider with transfer function

$$H(j\omega) \;=\; \frac{v_o}{v_i} \;=\; \frac{1}{1 + j\omega\, C_L\, Z_x(j\omega)}$$

3. fs — motional series resonance

Defined intrinsically by the motional branch: $X_m(\omega_s) = 0$. Because $X_m$ depends only on $L_m$ and $C_m$, $R_m$ cannot move it.

$$\boxed{\;f_s \;=\; \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{L_m C_m}}\;}$$

4. fr and fp — zero-phase crossings of Zx

The two roots of $\operatorname{Im}\{Z_x\} = 0$ correspond to the whole crystal being purely resistive. The denominator of $\operatorname{Im}\{Z_x\}$ is strictly positive, so the numerator must vanish:

$$X_m^{\,2} + X_m X_0 + R_m^{\,2} \;=\; 0$$

Substituting $X_0 = -1/(\omega C_0)$:

$$\omega^{2}\, C_0\,\bigl(X_m^{\,2} + R_m^{\,2}\bigr) \;=\; X_m$$

Now let $X_m = \omega L_m - 1/(\omega C_m)$, $y = \omega^{2}$, and multiply through by $y\, C_m^{\,2}\, C_0$. Every non-polynomial piece cancels and an exact quadratic in $y$ remains:

$$A_1\, y^{2} + B_1\, y + C_1 \;=\; 0$$ $$A_1 = (L_m C_m)^{2} \;,\quad B_1 = R_m^{\,2} C_m^{\,2} - L_m C_m\!\left(2 + \tfrac{C_m}{C_0}\right) \;,\quad C_1 = 1 + \tfrac{C_m}{C_0}$$ $$\boxed{\;y_{\pm} \;=\; \frac{-B_1 \pm \sqrt{B_1^{\,2} - 4\, A_1\, C_1}}{2\, A_1}, \quad f_r \;=\; \frac{\sqrt{y_-}}{2\pi}, \quad f_p \;=\; \frac{\sqrt{y_+}}{2\pi}\;}$$

Rm→0 check: $B_1^{\,2} - 4 A_1 C_1 \;=\; (L_m C_m\cdot C_m/C_0)^{2}$ exactly, so $f_r \to f_s$ and $f_p \to f_s\sqrt{1 + C_m/C_0}$. ✓

5. fL — load resonance (oscillation frequency)

At the Pierce oscillation frequency, the crystal in series with $C_L$ must be purely resistive:

$$\operatorname{Im}\bigl\{Z_x(j\omega) + 1/(j\omega C_L)\bigr\} \;=\; 0 \;\;\Longleftrightarrow\;\; \operatorname{Im}\{Z_x\} \;=\; \frac{1}{\omega C_L}$$

Using the exact $\operatorname{Im}\{Z_x\}$ from §1, clearing the denominator, and letting $A \equiv X_m + X_0$:

$$C_0\, A^{2} + C_L\, X_m\, A + (C_0 + C_L)\, R_m^{\,2} \;=\; 0$$

Substituting $A = X_m - 1/(\omega C_0)$, $X_m = \omega L_m - 1/(\omega C_m)$, and $y = \omega^{2}$, collecting powers of $y$, gives a second exact quadratic:

$$A_2\, y^{2} + B_2\, y + C_2 \;=\; 0$$ $$A_2 \;=\; (C_0 + C_L)\, C_0\, (L_m C_m)^{2}$$ $$B_2 \;=\; -\,L_m C_m\bigl[\,2(C_0+C_L)\,C_0 + (2C_0 + C_L)\,C_m\,\bigr] \;+\; (C_0 + C_L)\, C_0\, C_m^{\,2}\, R_m^{\,2}$$ $$C_2 \;=\; C_0\,(C_0 + C_L) \;+\; C_m\,(2C_0 + C_L + C_m)$$ $$\boxed{\;f_L \;=\; \frac{\sqrt{y_-}}{2\pi}\,,\qquad y_- \;=\; \frac{-B_2 - \sqrt{B_2^{\,2} - 4\, A_2\, C_2}}{2\, A_2}\;}$$

Rm→0 check: $B_2^{\,2} - 4 A_2 C_2 \;=\; (C_m C_L)^{2}$ exactly, so $f_L \to f_s\sqrt{1 + C_m/(C_0 + C_L)}$. ✓
